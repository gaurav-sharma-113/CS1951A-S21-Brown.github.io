Where is the data from?

  • How did you collect your data?

We collected the data from two kaggle repositories here and here. They were the Rotten Tomatoes - Movies and Critics Dataset and The Movies Dataset.

  • Is the source reputable?

Kaggle is a preeminent source for data science repositories. Many researchers at universities and in industry rely on their services.

  • How did you generate the sample? Is it comparably small or large? Is it representative or is it likely to exhibit some kind of sampling bias?

The sample is created by joining The Movies Dataset with the Rotten Tomatoes dataset yielding 1909 movie datapoints. Both original datasets and the resulting joined dataset seem diverse in terms of genre and movie budget which in conjunction with the relatively large dataset size indicates that there will be less sampling bias.

  • How many data points are there total? How many are there in each group you care about (e.g. if you are dividing your data into positive/negative examples, are they split evenly)? Do you think this is enough data to do what you hope to do?

There are 1909 data points that we were left with after we joined our datasets. Since we plan to analyze how factors (such as budget, release date, genre) influence a movie’s success, we made sure to have substantial data across all of these spectrums. Considering most of the movies in our dataset have relatively high budgets (since these are the ones that hit theaters and had audience reviews), almost all of our data will be relevant.

  • Are there missing values? Do these occur in fields that are important for your project's goals?

There are no missing values in the final cleaned dataset although there were data points/rows where movies have a listed zero dollar budget which appears to be obviously inaccurate. We removed those data points post joining since having a non-zero budget is important to our project’s goals/stats.

  • Are there duplicates? Do these occur in fields that are important for your project's goals?

There appears to be no duplicates although there are “duplicates” in the sense that different movies may have the same movie title. We overcame this issue by joining on both movie name and release date to join unique movies across both datasets.

  • How is the data distributed? Is it uniform or skewed? Are there outliers? What are the min/max values? (focus on the fields that are most relevant to your project goals)

There is some skew present within the data. More films for instance have lower budgets as only a few films have the funding to reach larger budget levels and only in more recent years have there been massive blockbuster films. Thus there is a minor skew towards lower budget films. The main outliers were movies with zero budget and/or revenue. These are mostly older films and likely indicate that there is not enough clean data to make these movies usable to run statistics on. We have already cleaned out these outliers from the final dataset.

  • Are there any data type issues (e.g. words in fields that were supposed to be numeric)? Where are these coming from? (E.g. a bug in your scraper? User input?) How will you fix them?

The only data type issues are that the release dates need to be converted from string format into datetime data. Additionally all numeric values need to be converted from strings.

  • Do you need to throw any data away? What data? Why? Any reason this might affect the analyses you are able to run or the conclusions you are able to draw?

We threw away data in that we got rid of movies that were not in both databases. We are also throwing away data for movies that have inconsistent or nonsensical data, such as 0 dollar budgets.

Summarize any challenges or observations you have made since collecting your data. Then, discuss your next steps and how your data collection has impacted the type of analysis you will perform. (approximately 3-5 sentences)

Some challenges with our data is that although both databases that we used have a large cross-section in terms of the movies they contain, the movies are sometimes represented slightly differently, meaning there are sometimes slight inconsistencies in name and release date. Next steps are to make use of Levenshtein Edit Distance to group rows together that represent the same movie but have slightly differing titles. We also checked for release dates within a short span of one another just to double check that the two movies are indeed the same.
