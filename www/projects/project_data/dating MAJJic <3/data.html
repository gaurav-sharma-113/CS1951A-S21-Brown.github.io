Dating MAJJic - Tech Report

Our Questions and Hypotheses:

  1. How does your college major predict who you are initially attracted to?
    Does this differ between male/female preferences?
  1. For females there is no correlation between college major and attractiveness to another specific college major, but males are more attracted to people within their major.
  1. How does your college major predict who you end up marrying?
    Does this differ between male/female preferences?
  1. No correlation for both female and male
  1. How does your intended career predict who you are initially attracted to?
    Does this differ between male/female preferences?
  1. No correlation for both female and male
  1. How does your occupation predict who you end up marrying?
    Does this differ between male/female preferences?
  1. Both males and females tend to marry someone within the same occupation.
  1. How does your initial attraction to someone based on their college major compare to who you end up marrying? Does this differ between male/female preferences?
  1. The college majors you are initially attracted are unrelated to the college majors you end up marrying
  1. How does your initial attraction to someone based on their intended career compare to who you end up marrying in terms of their actual occupation?
    Does this differ between male/female preferences?
  1. The intended careers of the people you are initially attracted to are consistent with  the occupation of the person you end up marrying

Dataset 1: Kaggle Speed Dating Data

Description of Data:

How did you collect your data?

The data was collected by a research group led by Professors Ray Fisman and Sheena Iyengar from Columbia University Business School. The research team performed a speed dating experiment to determine what factors influence an individual’s degree of attraction for another individual during a date and the likelihood of the two individuals going on a second date. Following the conclusion of the study, the research group made the collected data publicly available on Kaggle (https://www.kaggle.com/annavictoria/speed-dating-experiment), where we downloaded it in hopes of performing our data science project.

The procedures for consolidating and extracting the relevant data is detailed in a later section, but the information provided by the dataset that we were interested in working with were as follows: IID (individual ID), PID (partner ID), age, race, gender, dec (final decision score for their partner), attr (attractiveness score for their partner), sinc (sincerity score for their partner), intel (intelligence score for their partner), fun (humor score for their partner), amb (ambition score for their partner), shar (shared interests score for their partner), like (overall likeability score for their partner), and prob (probability of meeting again score for their partner).

Is the source reputable?

We consider the Columbia University Business School to be a fairly reputable source for data, seeing as they are an established academic institution. Furthermore, the data was used as the basis for findings published in the article “Gender Differences in Mate Selection: Evidence from a Speed Dating Experiment” authored by the investigators and leaders of the investigation, Professor Raymond Fisman and Professor Sheena S. Iyengar of Columbia University. The article can be found in the journal The Quarterly Journal of Economics, which has a very high impact factor of 11.78. Finally, the data is available to the public on Kaggle, a public forum where users can provide their own insights into the legitimacy of the data. The dataset has over 210,000 views and 35,900 downloads, with very few concerns brought up in the user discussion section, which gives us confidence in using this data as a component of our final project.

How did you generate the sample? Is it comparably small or large? Is it representative or is it likely to exhibit some kind of sampling bias?

The sample is taken from the aforementioned speed dating experiment, uploaded to Kaggle.  The sample size is somewhat large (551 individuals surveyed) for an in-person experiment, but comparably small relative to our corresponding census data set.

Whether or not the data is representative depends on who may be looking to translate it and apply its implications to themselves—the sample comes from graduate students at Columbia University, so the findings from the data may be relevant to the populace at another prestigious academic institution on the East Coast (such as Brown University or Harvard University), but may have more of a bias when viewed by people living in a rural city in the American Midwest or South. Another potential bias is that the data was collected in ‘waves’, and as such some waves are dominated by certain groups (field of study or profession) with other groups having few representatives in attendance.

Cleaning the Data / Initial Analysis:

The dataset contains everything we needed--it records the experience/rating for each speed date that each individual experienced, the individual’s personal and demographic information, and the partner’s personal and demographic information. This provides us the information necessary to test our hypotheses, specifically the components related to initial attraction. For example, using this information, we can see whether individuals of a certain field of study rated a dating experience with another individual from the same field of study as higher, lower, or no difference relative to individuals of a different field of study.

However, while the documentation for the variables and the categorizations are very thorough, the data is organized in a way that is very difficult to interpret and perform analyses. The data is presented so that each entry is a “dating experience”, meaning many different entries/rows correspond to the same individual. Therefore, it was necessary to consolidate all the ratings of each date that an individual went on into one single entry/row. All of the data was fortunately numeric so there was no need to fix any data type issues. There were unfortunately missing values for some individuals, in which case we set those to NULL values so that we would not make any incorrect assumptions that could skew the conclusions of our analyses.

While it may appear as though there are many NULL values in our final consolidated database, and it could seem like there is reason to be concerned, this is actually as expected given the experimental design of the investigation. Since multiple speed dating events were conducted over the span of several months, some participants were present at events where not all of the fields of studies or intended careers were represented. For example, a law student could have attended an event that had no biology PhD students present whatsoever. In which case, all of the students present would have NULL ratings for the biology field because there were no students to interact with. We have performed sanity checks on the calculations and consolidation methodology/algorithms we executed in our code to ensure that we were computing the correct average scores and totals, and we are confident that the new average scores/ratings are representative of what each student rated the corresponding party. Procedure for cleaning - Kaggle Speed Dating Data:

Here is the procedure we used to clean the dataset:

  1. For every individual participant, the individual ID (iid), field of study (field_cd), intended career (career_c), age (age), and race (race) was extracted and recorded.
  1. The following iid’s did not possess an affiliated field_cd, career_c, age, and race:
  1. 58, 59, 118, 136, 339, 340, 346
  2. This represents 1.3% of our  (7/551)
  1. The data retrieved above was stored inside a sql table named: demographics
  2. When rating a speed date, there are 9 different scoring categories. The categories are as follow:
  1. Final decision (dec: 1 for ‘yes to another date’, 0 for ‘no to another date’)
  2. Attractiveness (attr: Scale of 1-10, with 10 being the highest)
  3. Sincerity (sinc: Scale of 1-10, with 10 being the highest)
  4. Intelligence (intel: Scale of 1-10, with 10 being the highest)
  5. Funniness (fun: Scale of 1-10, with 10 being the highest)
  6. Ambition (amb: Scale of 1-10, with 10 being the highest)
  7. Shared Interests (shar: Scale of 1-10, with 10 being the highest)
  8. Overall interest (like: Scale of 1-10, with 10 being the highest)
  9. Probability of meeting again (prob: Scale of 1-10, with 10 being the highest)
  1. For the categories mentioned above, create a variable for each of the categories for each of the possible fields of undergraduate study. Essentially, each field of study will have a corresponding rating (i.e. law_dec, law_attr, law_intel,..., bio_dec, bio_attr...etc.)
  2. Iterate through every speed date that was recorded. Determine the iid of the person who provided the rating. Then determine the individual ID of the partner being rated (pid) and the field_cd of the partner being rated. Then, add the score to the iid’s rating of their partner’s corresponding field. For example, if iid #1 went on a date with pid #12, who is a lawyer, then iid #1’s score for pid #12 will be added to the following categories: law_dec, law_attr, law_intel, law_fun, law_amb, law_shar, law_like, law_prob. Once all of iid #1’s dates have been iterated through, the scores will be averaged depending on how many individuals from that field of study they went on a date with.
  3. At the conclusion of the entire iteration, each iid should have an average score for each field of study in each of the possible scoring categories.
  1. If the partner did not have a field of study indicated, then the score for that person was not added to any category because we did not want to make assumptions off of the person.
  2. During the speed dating experiment, it is almost guaranteed that each iid did not see an individual from every possible field of study. Therefore, there will be fields of study that an iid did not rate. In those cases, those fields and their scores were left as None values in Python and NULL values in SQL.
  1. Steps 3-6 were then repeated for the intended careers (career_c) demographics category.
  2. The field of study data was stored in a sql table named “field_of_study”, and the intended careers data was stored in a sql table named “career”.
  3. The tables “demographics”, “field_of_study”, and “career” can be found in the file: “kaggle_clean.db”

Challenges and Limitations

Our first challenge was finding the dataset--the original ACS PUMS data does not group each person with their spouse, so we tried to find a way to use the other data to pair individuals with their spouses. However, we later were able to find the IPUMS dataset that does this grouping for us.

The main challenge was grouping the college degree and occupation codes specified in the census data to fit the categories in the Kaggle dataset. We first manually assigned all of the education codes from the census data a degree category from the kaggle dataset. To do so, we created a table that stored these pairs and then added another column to the original dataset containing the matching kaggle code based on the pairs from the table. This column was called kagEDUC for the original person and kagEDUC_SP for their spouse. To group the occupation codes specified in the census data, we again created a table where we matched a range of occupation codes to each kaggle occupation. Using this table, we created two more columns in the original dataset where kagOCC contains the matching occupation of the person and kagOCC_SP contains the matching occupation of the spouse from the kaggle dataset.

We also realized that there are some limitations of our dataset. Firstly, the DEGFIELD category only gives us information on the individual’s Bachelor degree, whereas the Kaggle dataset gives information on the person’s current degree (the study was conducted on students in graduate/professional schools). Thus, the degrees we are comparing are not exactly the same. Secondly, the census data has 2 DEGFIELD categories (if the person had a second major). However, since the Kaggle dataset only has one college major per individual, we are just using the census data’s first DEGFIELD category. Lastly, the Kaggle dataset is from a study conducted between 2002-2004; we wanted to use the same years for the ;census data, but during these years the DEGFIELD category was not available. Thus, we used the closest available years (2009-2010).


Next Steps

Initially, we were interested in comparing categories other than just college major and occupation, such as race and income. However, the census dataset is so large and detailed that we didn’t realize how much work would go into fitting the occupation/major codes into the Kaggle dataset’s categorizations. Thus, we decided to limit our analysis and focus on just college major and occupation. In addition, we thought there might be a difference in preference for different genders. However, both the Kaggle dataset and the census data only categorize genders under male/female, so our data will be limited to these two genders.

Our next step is to begin performing statistical analyses so that we can prove or disprove our hypotheses and generate content for the interactive component of the final project. As of right now, we intend on utilizing a form of linear regression modeling where the outcome we are predicting is one of the immediate dating scores provided by the Kaggle Speed Dating Experiment data (i.e. attractiveness, overall likeability, probability of going out on a second date...etc.) and the covariates/predicting variables are the individual’s and partner’s respective category of interest (i.e. college major and occupation). We will then repeat this process for the US Census Data. After determining whether a covariate is a significant or insignificant predictor of immediate attraction and dating likelihood, we will then test whether the result holds true in marriage by comparing it with the analysis for US Census Data. This will most likely be done through a difference of means statistical test, where we will attempt to see whether there is a significant representation of the correlation, or lack thereof, that we saw in the Kaggle dataset within the actual population of married individuals.
