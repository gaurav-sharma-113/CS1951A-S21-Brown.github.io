Prediction:

We predict that for each factor below there is a correlation between the factor and the savings rate of a country. Below describes the type of correlation and our very wild assumptions that lead to it.

For each factor:

  • Unemployment rate, in %: Ratio of unemployed population to labor force
  • Negative, assuming people will rely on savings while unemployed.
  • Male-to-population ratio, in %: Ratio of male population to the overall population
  • Negative, assuming males don’t really save.
  • Female-to-population ratio, in %: Ratio of female population to the overall population
  • Positive, assuming females save.
  • Total population: Number of people
  • Negative, assuming larger the population the larger the nominal GDP, but the increase in the nominal GDP is larger than the increase in the savings rate.
  • Population density: Number of people per square kilometres of land area
  • Negative, with the similar assumption as the total population factor.
  • Nominal GDP, in current local currency units relative to the U.S. dollar
  • Negative, assuming it is usually the case that the increase in nominal GDP is largely related to the increase in population, not the increase in the income
  • Nominal GDP per capita, in current local currency units relative to the U.S. dollar
  • Positive, assuming people with larger income has the tendency to save more
  • GDP growth, in %
  • Negative, assuming the stable GDP growth would give people more incentive to spend more
  • Age dependency ratio, young, in %
  • Negative, assuming people at young age have less knowledge on savings
  • Literacy rate, in % of population ages 15 and above
  • Positive, assuming higher literacy rate corresponds to higher number of people who understand the benefit of savings better
  • Education, in % of population 25 and above: Educational attainment of Doctoral or equivalent
  • Positive, assuming people with education have a better understanding in savings

  1. Linear regression

We perform linear regression on each of the independent variables, deciding whether or not each independent variable has a correlation with the dependent variable based on the results summary. Since we are planning to have the data points split into training and testing sets, and the fact that there are some null data points in the csv file, we made sure we have enough data to test on to get reliable results. In order to achieve that we concatenated the data from each year from 2010 to 2018 for each country, so that all the data from those years are combined and represents a single country. We are making an assumption that for each factor there is a high collinearity among the years, i.e. data in 2018 is highly related to the data in the previous eight years.

Using these methods we measure our model by p-value and R-squared value. If p-value falls inside the 95% interval, then we accept the relation, otherwise we will not consider this attribute, and conclude that the factor has no relationship with the savings rate. We will also use the R-squared value to measure the prediction accuracy of our regression for each attribute.

We perform our testing by two ways: t-testing, using t score, and linear regression. For the regression part, we give importance on the p-value, R-squared values and correlation coefficient. We chose these four values because we believed that they were the most indicative of how strongly our test supports accepting/rejecting our null hypothesis.

We considered incorporating the use of multiple linear regression, however, using the factors as the independent variables did not align with our prediction statement, which was to see if each of the attributes had a correlation, not all at once. Also, we used single regression to consider the correlation of each attribute with savings rate not only because we want to avoid explicit problems such as Simpson paradox, but also simple regression is always less fitted than multi-regression, and we don't want to risk losing too much data.

Another thing we need to improve next is the autocorrelation between different data, which is measured by the Durbin-Watson value. If we performed regression here, we need to assume that all randomly chosen data are not related to each other. For example, we could treat "savings 2000" and "savings 2018" as different rows which might have some autocorrelation between y_train and y_test data. The D-W value for a suitable model is considered to be between 1.5 and 2.5, but for some attributes, our value is less than 1.5.
