Stats and Visualization Report

Our goal

  • For our analysis we decided to look to use flight traffic data alongside early case reports of NCoV-2019. We defined a metric we called viral pressure that was the sum of the traffic into each country weighted by the number of cases per population unit in the originating country of the route. That is, for a country, its viral pressure is the sum for each inbound flight route of the number of passengers on that flight route multiplied by the number of cases from the originating country.
  • We hypothesized that our viral pressure metric might correlate negatively with the number of days to first infection for a country.
  • For each day since the beginning of the coronavirus pandemic, we ran a regression with days to first infection as the dependent variable and viral pressure as the  independent variable for each country.

Statistical Analysis

Methods

Why did you use this statistical test or ML algorithm? Which other tests did you consider or evaluate? How did you measure success or failure? Why that metric/value? What challenges did you face evaluating the model? Did you have to clean or restructure your data?

  • We wanted to see how a change in viral pressure affected the trend for days to infection. We used linear regression because we were looking for a correlation and only had a single metric.
  • We considered including more variables and running a multivariate regression, but we decided that there weren’t any related and readily available metrics that we could think of using.
  • We would consider this to be successful if we found that for a certain period of time during the spread of the pandemic there was a significant correlation. We weren’t certain where to set the bar for the existence of a correlation. That is, what R-squared value would signify that we had successfully found a relationship. However, it’s clear that with a maximum R-squared value of 0.06 we were not able to find a correlation with the data we had. That is unsurprising given the lack of flight traffic data.
  • Our data was the predominant challenge. Since we only had data for the top 100 flight routes, we ended with only five countries having any viral pressure at all.

Results

What is your interpretation of the results? Do accept or deny the hypothesis, or are you satisfied with your prediction accuracy? For prediction projects, we expect you to argue why you got the accuracy/success metric you have. Intuitively, how do you react to the results? Are you confident in the results?

  • For the countries with non-zero viral pressure, viral pressure increases exponentially.
  • Beyond this, our results are inconclusive. Our overall R-squared was very low at just 0.009 and the peak daily R-squared was 0.06.
  • An explanation for our probable inaccuracy is that our flight data was limited to 100 most common routes, and this resulted in only 5 countries with non-zero viral pressure values.
  • We are certainly not confident in our results. To make up for this, we’ve outlined other analyses we plan to undertake in the Next Steps section. We hope these tests and predictions will be better suited to the data we have.
