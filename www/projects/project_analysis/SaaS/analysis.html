Articles that contain the terms, “healthcare”, “woman”, “world”, and “connect” tend to be more liberal whereas articles that contain the terms, “veteran”, “demand”, “fight”, and “ceo” tend to be more conservative

H0: In a t-test, there is no statistically significant difference in the frequency of “healthcare”, “woman”, “world”, “connect”, “veteran”, “demand”, “fight”, and “ceo” in liberal versus conservative articles
HA: In a t-test, there is a statistically significant difference in the frequency of “healthcare”, “woman”, “world”, “connect”, “veteran”, “demand”, “fight”, and “ceo” in liberal versus conservative articles.

Why did you use this statistical test or ML algorithm? Which other tests did you consider or evaluate? How did you measure success or failure? Why that metric/value? What challenges did you face evaluating the model? Did you have to clean or restructure your data?

We used a t-test (a statistical test) because it is useful in finding if there is a statistically significant difference between two groups - in this case, liberal versus conservative articles. We also considered a chi squared test and correlation tests, such as Pearson and Spearman. We ultimately decided to choose the t-test over chi-squared because a t-test compares means in different categories whereas a chi-squared test is categorical, i.e. there are predetermined categories that the data will fall into. In this case, our categories were the political inclination of the media source of the articles, in which articles were labeled from -2 to 2, with negative being liberal and positive being conservative and the magnitude being an indicator of how strong the inclination was. We decided to use a statistical test rather than an ML algorithm because we are conducting hypothesis testing, in which we are inferring relationships between variables, as opposed to making predictions.
We measured success by determining whether or not we were rejecting the null hypothesis for each of the terms. We set the p-value to be 0.1. If the result from our t-test was less than our set threshold p-value of 0.1, then we rejected the null hypothesis in favour of the alternative hypothesis, indicating that the difference in the number of occurrences of the specific term in liberal vs conservative articles was statistically significant. If our t-test was higher than our set threshold p-value, we accepted the null hypothesis. We used this metric because it was convenient to replicate the test and perform it on any term in the list of the articles’ terms.
To ensure that our test was fair, we used a control term of “Thursday” to show that a word that we thought was politically neutral would not show a statistically significant difference in the number of times it appeared in liberal vs conservative articles.
A challenge we faced was determining the threshold p-value to use because we found that the p-values generated after performing the t-test on several of the terms fell in the 0.05 to 0.1 range, and using a threshold p-value of 0.05 would lead to the same conclusion for most of the terms chosen in our hypothesis: we would accept the null hypothesis. Similarly, we had to normalize our data because there were six times as many words from liberal articles than conservative articles. Conservative articles were in general underrepresented compared to liberal articles (approximately 1100 liberal articles versus 500 conservative articles). Therefore, we normalized the data to account for this difference; otherwise, the terms would more likely be associated with liberal articles simply because there are about double the number of liberal articles as conservative ones in the dataset.

We performed a t-test and calculated a p-value for each of the words. Our results show that five (“connect”, “demand”, “fight”, “veteran”, “world”) out of the eight terms chosen support our alternative hypothesis that there is a statistically significant difference in the frequency of these terms in liberal versus conservative articles. Three out of the eight terms (“ceo”, “healthcar”, “woman”) support our null hypothesis that there is no statistically significant difference in the frequency of these terms in liberal versus conservative articles. Terms such as “connect”, “fight”, “veteran”, and “world” tended to appear more in liberal articles whereas terms such as “demand” tended to appear more in conservative articles. Terms such as “ceo”, “healthcar”, and “woman” are more neutral, not appearing significantly more in liberal or conservative articles. We believe we got these results since terms like “connect” and “world” are often associated with globalism (connecting with other countries, specifically when creating foreign and economic policy), which is a more liberal ideal. In addition, “ceo” and “woman” are both fairly neutral words, not particularly specific to liberal or conservative ideology.
We are fairly confident in our results, but using a p-value less than 0.1 such as 0.05 would indicate that there is a closer relationship between our chosen terms and liberal or conservative articles.
