PROJECT SUMMARY

Our project is an attempt to create useful models for representing the political leanings of followers of newspaper accounts on twitter (e.g. @BostonGlobe, @nytimes). Given a twitter follower of a newspaper, we use an external database of common political twitter accounts with associated political scores, see which of these ‘political’ accounts they are following, and then aggregate the associated scores to give that newspaper follower a political leaning score. For example, on one of the political accounts datasets, a score of (-2) is very liberal and (2) very conservative, loosely.** For a given newspaper, the distribution of these scores allow us to approximate a probability density function f(x) which, for a given paper, tells us the probability that a follower has a political score of ‘x’. We find these pdfs to be useful models of the political followings of a given newspaper, ones that are both useful for classification and easily visualized.

** Our pipeline is flexible enough to take these outside political accounts and associated political leaning scores from multiple sources. For this checkpoint, we used three outside datasets:

  1. A list of political accounts and scores formulated in the paper Birds of the Same Feather Tweet Together: Bayesian Ideal Point Estimation Using Twitter Data by Pablo Barbera. These scores are continuous where a score of (-2) is very liberal and (2) very conservative, loosely
  2. Every politician tracked by ProPublica in the U.S., with a score of (-1) if they are Democrat and (+1) if they are Republican
  3. The top 200 Twitter accounts liberals follow most and the top 200 accounts most followed by Republicans (also from Barbera’s paper). Because of this flexibility, we may extend or aggregate these scores in the future.

CLASSIFICATION PROBLEM

We wanted a classification problem that both showed the efficacy of our pdf models and gave us the flexibility to experiment and see what features of our models were most useful. To this end, we decided to use a decision tree to classify our pdfs into one of five classes, from most liberal to most conservative, where the true class labels come from previous work done by AllSides Media Bias ratings.

Decision trees are one of the most widely used supervised classification methods for machine learning and have many advantages. When the model trains it is able to sort through a lot of variables to find the ones that correlate best with the desired results, and can be modified with a maximum depth and a train test split to prevent overfitting. The method works better than linear regression for classification, since it already creates the threshold for each classification. In addition, decision trees are one of the more clearly explainable classification methods. That being said, we considered using both linear and logistic regression as an alternative method of predicting the data, and doing this will be one of our next steps.

We measure success or failure by looking at the percent accuracy of our model in the test data set of our train test split. To implement this test we bootstrapped the results of the decision tree to run it 10,000 times with 10% of the data as the testing set. In this way we could have less variation in the results and be more confident in our results. We picked this metric because ultimately our goal is to find the model that best predicts the polarization of papers, so we optimized our model for this results. We did it on the test part of our data to avoid overfitting. There were a couple different challenges we had with evaluating our model. First, we only had around 50 scored papers from All Sides, so getting one more paper correct, would be a significant improvement in the model. Along those lines, it was kind  of arbitrary which variables we selected to use to optimize our model. This has the risk of succumbing to overfitting, where certain variables just happen to do better based on our specific data. Additionally we had to decide what types or errors we cared about most. The model that did best with five classifications, didn’t scale as well when we reduced the data to three classifications.

To clean the data we had to run a variety of SQL queries to pull all the different potential fields to run our model on. We sent these results to a csv, which was then passed into the decision tree model. We also experimented with averaging the results of all three models of political leaning, and controlling for number of politicians people follow, but neither of these improved the model (See “Accounting for Politician Following Count” for more info).

ACCOUNTING FOR POLITICIAN FOLLOWING COUNT

One way we tried to restructure our data, was to account for the relatively large number of people who the only politician they followed was Donald Trump. Trump receives a twitter polarization score near zero, since so many people following him, including many people who don’t support him, but his presence in the graph still makes a big difference. You can see in the graph in Other Visualizations 1 on the left, one bar representing Trump stands out. To account for this a bit, we weight every user by the cube root of the number of politicians they follow. This way people that only follow one politician are not weighted as highly. The graph on the right is the altered version. Trump still sticks out but not nearly as much.

INTERPRETATION OF RESULTS

Our results show that our model is fairly accurate at predicting the biases of a paper. I think we are satisfied with the accuracy of our result because we were able to predict this result by only looking at 200 readers of a paper. For a lot of these national papers that are classified as far right or far left, they are still read by millions of people so they have a much more mainstream audience than their rating implies. For example, Huffington Post is considered a far left paper, and the New York Post is considered a far right paper. However, both of these news organizations are well regarded by the general public and read by many people. Therefore, it is a difficult task for our model to predict the political leaning of a paper just from looking at 200 readers who might be all across the political spectrum.

Intuitively, the results look correct. The far right American talk show host Alex Jones was correctly characterized as extremely conservative by our model. Similarly, AlterNet is a far left progressive paper that often mixes fact and opinion, according to their Wikipedia page, and our model picked up on that. It also seems correct that, in general, most papers are slightly liberal. This is because research shows that people who read the news are on average slightly more liberal than the average American, and that Twitter users are more liberal than the general public.

Diving more into the specifics of the data we see that the are multiple versions of the model that do fairly well at predicting the political leaning of papers. Using all the possible data, the model classifies the papers correctly about 50% of the time in the test sample when optimizing the depth of the tree (randomness would yield 20% results). When optimizing the variables we can get up to around 64% (16,19 combination) accuracy with five classifications, and 86% (2, 5 combination) with 3 classifications. The question then be comes, which model is optimal. In looking at the confusion matrices of these models (the two 5x5 grids below the pure data), we see that the (2,5) combination confuses the difference between far left and center left media more often, while the (16,19) combination confuses the difference between center left and center media more often. We care more about the difference between center and center left, so based on this, the (2,5) combination yields the best decision tree.

WHY DO WE THINK WE GOT STRONG RESULTS?

We believe that we got strong results for several reasons:

  1. We had several criteria to filter when selecting 200 random followers to ensure that they were normal people and not bots. These criteria included:
  1. Their names didn’t include the top 1000 common english words
  2. They followed at least 25 people
  3. Their names didn’t include numbers, special characters, or all caps
  1. We had three different labelled datasets of politicians, and we tested all three to see which was the most accurate. The first dataset was a set of political twitter accounts that were weighted on a continuous scale by a research paper that ranked political accounts. The second dataset was a much larger number of accounts that were the official twitter campaign accounts of state and national politicians. The last dataset was the 200 most followed accounts by liberals and conservatives, and this was also from the first paper. We used a decision tree to decide on the best features to use between all of these input datasets that we provided.
  2. We are weighting our results to help smooth out the distributions:
  1. Regression to the mean - We are more confident about the political leaning of people who follow more politicians. Therefore, we are adding three zeros to the political score of a user before averaging. This means that a user who follows more politicians will be weighted more highly because the three zeros matter less when taking an average.

FUTURE DIRECTIONS

I think our visualization with the distributions of papers from a -3 to +3 scale is slightly challenging to follow. There’s no clear interpretation of what a score of “-1” means besides slightly liberal, so we want to come up with some way to give readers an intuitive understanding of what that score means. We could potentially do that by plotting different politicians on that line to give readers a reference, but our scored values for politicians correlate more closely what the effect of following a politician demonstrates about the person following that account than the politician’s actual political values. For example, Trump’s score is near zero for our model even though he is a fairly conservative politician because so many people on Twitter follow Trump that following him is not particularly revealing of someone’s politics. We need to come up with a better way to give viewers an intuitive understanding of these scores.

Another future direction we could take this is trying to smooth our distributions for each paper by removing the “Trump Effect”, which is how we refer to the very large number of users for whom the only politician they follow is Trump. We could do this in several ways:

  1. Remove Trump for our data - this option might make sense because his score is so close to 0 that it is not very revealing
  2. Adjusting our hyperparameter that weights users who follow multiple politicians more highly. Maybe we should multiply by the square root of the number of politicians someone follows instead of the cube root, or maybe the function should be a sigmoid where we have extremely low confidence from just 1 politician but anything more than 7 politicians is generally high confidence.

Finally, we want to try more classification algorithms, given their relative merits and drawbacks. For example, we may get higher accuracy with a logistic regression, or we may be able to more clearly quantify correlation values for different variables with a linear regression.
