Analysis

Hypotheses

After evidence that Facebook could have prevented election interference in the 2016 election, and the subsequent Cambridge Analytica Data Breach in early 2018, the practices of data collection and targeted advertising by tech companies in general were made more visible to the general public. As a result of this bad press during and after the 2016 election cycle, our general question is: How, if at all, did tech companies’ role in political fundraising change after the 2016 election cycle? To do this, we are examining the donations by the PACs associated with tech companies.

  • Our hypothesis was that, after negative press in 2016, tech companies became “more politically active” in order to save their image, in that the average amount donated by PACs to various campaigns increased. We will be able to reject the null hypothesis if there is a significant difference in donation trends before and after 2016, controlling for other possible confounding factors.

  • Our secondary hypothesis was that companies’ donations would skew conservative on average, especially after discussions of regulating tech companies after the Cambridge Analytica breach. If this were the case, we would see that party affiliation of specific candidates would be a significant effect on donation amount.

Statistical Tests

We used multiple linear regression to test our hypothesis, because we felt it was the best way to take into account multiple possibly confounding variables and also to take time into account, since our data has a strong time component. We considered a correlation analysis but ultimately felt it would be too simplistic for the data we have.

We measure success or failure of our analysis by whether or not we were able to observe significant effects in our data without over- or under-fitting our data. We consider significant effects to be those with P values below 0.05, especially those with large coefficients. This would mean that given a confidence level of 95%, the results would be significant.

In evaluating the model, one challenge was that we collected 2020 data in our table, but this data is incomplete and was therefore skewing our results. We decided to leave this data out of our final analysis and use it only for visualization and total donation calculation purposes. We had to significantly restructure our data – for example, we had to split state data into regions (we chose census regions), control for various different races (i.e. House, Senate, and Presidential), and reformat the year. We also had to import our data into a CSV file, which made it easier to use pandas to read it in.

For Table 1, We incorporated 5 independent variables("year", "is_after_2016", "senate", "democratic", "west" corresponding to x1-x6 in the table above.) and a constant to explain the dependent variable(“donation amount”). We found the variable “is_after_2016”, “senate”,”democratic”, and “west” to be statistically significant with a p-value of 0.011, 0.000, 0.003, and 0.000 respectively, given a confidence level of 95%. This means if the donation year is after 2016, the donation amount decreases by 205.4760 dollars, this seems to be working against our hypothesis. If the race is senate instead of house or national, the donation amount increases by 415.7236 dollars. If the candidate is a democrat, the donation is likely to decrease by 137.7657 dollars. If the donation is given to a candidate located in the West(Alaska, California, Hawaii, Oregon, Washington. Arizona, Colorado, Idaho, Montana, Nevada, New Mexico, Utah, and Wyoming), the donation amount increases by 328.5509 dollars.

However, when we include a variable to account for whether the donation is in an election year in Table 2, the new variable makes “is_after_2016” insignificant, with a p-value of 0.890, which is greater than 0.05, given our confidence level of 95%. The addition of the election year variable showed us that it makes our ability to predict the effect of bad press on donations to be inconclusive. Perhaps incorporating more data after 2018 would help strengthen this statistical model.

We want to improve our prediction accuracy by incorporating future donation data perhaps from 2020, this may give us a statistically significant p-value to conclude about the effect of pre/post 2016. However, we also can see from both Table 1 and visualization of democratic and republican party donation trends over time that there is a decrease if not an increase in donation amount. These data make us believe that we lean toward rejecting our hypothesis.

Reflection

  • If you did a statistics test, are there any confounding trends or variables you might be observing?
    • Our test shows that there is not a significant relationship between whether or not the election was after 2016 and the amount of each donation. However, this could be explained by the fact that we only have one midterm year’s worth of data post-2016, and therefore do not have a large enough quantity of data to see any trends.
    • Further, we conducted our analysis on the entire dataset of donations, which means we are analyzing based on each individual donation, rather than summing a company’s total donations. This means that our analysis actually looks at average donation size and whether or not companies donate larger amounts to given candidates at one time. This does not take into account the sum of tech companies’ donations or the total amount given to a candidate in a given year.
      • We may try to control for this by doing company-specific tests (e.g. for the “big five” or top ten companies), and we may do a few further broader stats tests for our final presentation and poster.
