Hypothesis, as a reminder (has changed since last due date, approved by Ellie): We believe that expert birders report sightings of common birds less often than non-experts do because they have a better ability to spot difficult-to-find birds, and know where uncommon birds are located. As such, their desire to report more common birds diminishes.

Tech Report

Where is the data from: Our data is collected from eBird.org, an online data collecting and analysis organization dedicated to bird sightings in the United States, run by the Cornell Lab of Ornithology. eBird collects self-reported data from millions of observers around the world about the species, location, and amount of birds sighted at a given time. Users report their sightings through a mobile app (or less commonly, the website), and all observational data is made publicly available for download and use.

Why this source is reputable: eBird is one of the leading ornithology databases in the world, and has been used for dozens of rigorous scientific studies. Though the data is self-reported, which introduces a number of biases that are the focus of our study, rare sightings are reviewed and verified by expert ornithologists.

How we generated the sample: We generated our sample by downloading all observations for 2019 for a specific region, denoted within the eBird Database. Our region covers the states of Minnesota, Michigan, Illinois, Wisconsin, Indiana, Iowa. Compared to the overall size of the eBird database, our selection is extremely small. We study around ~1 million data points, and the entire database has over 500 million data points. In addition, the full database spans back more than eight years. For our given time period and area, the data is representative of the sightings, though self-reported data may not be entirely accurate as to the true number of birds in the region, as we will focus on in our analysis. However,  some sub-analyses will randomly sample from our full dataset, and will thus not be fully representative at that time.

Data Cleanliness: Over time, eBird’s recording services have become stricter, allowing less information to be left out. Fortunately for us, this means the data is very clean. For example, whereas in the past, eBirders were allowed to mark that they had seen a bird without recording the quantity, quantities were required by 2019 and thus are always present in our dataset. Originally, our specific region in 2019 contained 4,313,330 data points. However, we needed to add specific habitat metrics to all data points in order to calculate each user’s expert score. As we were not able to access the full NASA database used to calculate the habitat metrics in the paper, we were only able to keep data points from 2019 that matched within three decimal places to one of the 10,195 latitude and longitude coordinates appearing in the paper’s data. This filtering brought our total number of records down to 1,642,205. Initial analyses on the distributions between the original dataset and our filtered-down version indicate we are left with a relatively representative sample of the data, as can be seen in the tables below.

Additionally, our analysis requires that we have a list of common and uncommon birds within the region. Unfortunately, no organization, eBird included, provides a clear list of birds in this format for the given region. Instead, the paper linked below includes an assessment of which birds were cited at similar rates by both experts and non-experts, and which birds had the greatest disparity between experts and non-experts from 2002-2012. We are using these two lists as common and uncommon birds, respectively. This proxy is not perfect — the disparity between expert and non-expert sightings may be explained by other factors, including the types of places experts go to look for birds, false positive and false negative reports, the size of the bird, how the bird nests, or even whether the bird often appears at feeders, among other possibilities. Additionally, the paper’s time frame is 7 years earlier than ours; bird populations may have shifted during this time period.  However, the proxy is the best that we can use at this time for the given region. Other considerations: When collecting our data, we were originally anticipating studying 2012-present for the given region, but were overwhelmed by the sheer number of data points (~10 million at the time). As a result, we restricted our collection process even further to just 2019, which gave the added benefit of cleaner data as the application introduced data cleaning and verification methods over time. All of the users are informed that their data will be made publicly available, thus avoiding any privacy issues for bird sightings.

Challenges and Future Steps: As noted previously, we were surprised at how much data there was for our given question, and had to limit our study as a result. We also had a difficult time replicating the expert scores for each user from the linked paper because the paper did not include code to do so, though our model was eventually successful. We would next like to look at correlations between types of bird sightings and expert scores, comparing and contrasting how experts sight the birds listed above vs. non-experts. The process of assigning expert scores gave us a greater intuition of what variables may be correlated with common bird sightings and which may not, which will help us narrow down and randomly sample in future experiments. Additionally, we would like to dive into other variations between experts and non experts, potentially looking into the locations from which they sight birds and how they differ (or not).

Column 0: Unique ID - URN:CornellLabOfOrnithology:EBIRD:OBS759857906

We plan to remove the first parts to isolate the actual ID and can use this as our primary key for each record.

Column 1: Taxonomic Order - 31739

The numeric value assigned to this taxon in the eBird/Clements taxonomy. Useful for ordering the file in the latest taxonomic sequence. Note that some have decimal values, so these should not be rounded off to whole numbers.

Column 2: Category - species

The category (e.g., species, hybrid, slash) assigned to this taxon in the eBird/Clements taxonomy. A full description of these categories is here: http://help.ebird.org/customer/portal/articles/1006825.

Column 3: Common Name - Clay-colored Sparrow

The primary English common name of the taxon in the eBird/Clements taxonomy. These are consistent (selected from a list rather than input by hand).

Column 4: Scientific Name - Spizella pallida

The scientific name of the taxon in the eBird/Clements taxonomy. Again, consistent.

Column 5: Subspecies Common Name - _

The common name of the subspecies or subspecies group in the eBird/Clements taxonomy. Certain other taxa (such as intergrades, and certain forms and domestics) also included here. This is frequently, if not mostly, empty.

Column 6: Subspecies Scientific Name - _

The scientific name of the subspecies or subspecies group in the eBird/Clements taxonomy. Certain other taxa (such as intergrades, and certain forms and domestics) also included here. Again, often null.

Column 7: Observation Count - 1

The count of individuals made at the time of observation. Note: In the past, if no count was made, an 'X' is used to indicate presence. This is not the case in data from 2019 to the present, but if we decide to use older data, then we will have to account for this.

Column 8: State - Minnesota

The state/province where the observation was made. Our data includes one region, made up of five states (Minnesota, Michigan, Illinois, Wisconsin, Indiana, Iowa). We will most likely not be using this column for much.

Column 9: County - Carver

The county where the observation was made.

Column 10: BCR CODE  - 23

 The alphanumeric code for a Bird Conservation Region. If an observation falls within a particular BCR, it is given this code. A list of codes and their corresponding site names is included in the metadata bundle (BCRCodes.txt). More on BCRs here: http://www.nabci-us.org/bcrs.htm. In accordance with the paper we are following, we will be using only area code 23. The data is downloaded by states, so one of our first steps is filtering to just BCR 23.

Column 11: Locality - Minnesota Landscape Arboretum

The reported location name for the observation. Observers can give locations their own names, or choose from existing locations (eBird Hotspots, see Explore Hotspots). This column might be dirty because users can write in their own responses.

Column 12: Locality ID - L1049490

Unique alphanumeric code for a location.

Column 13: Observation Date - 2019-05-15

Date of the observation expressed as year-month-day (YYYY-MM-DD).

Column 14: Observer ID - obsr182922

Unique number associated with each eBird observer.

Column 15: Is Stationary? - 0

Using the column “Protocol Type” in the data, this column is 1 if the observer marked stationary and 0 if not. Protocol Type is the type of survey associated with this sampling event.  The three main protocol types are Traveling Count, Stationary Count, and Casual Observation.

Column 16: Duration Minutes - 90.0

The duration of the sampling event reported in minutes.

Column 17: Effort Distance km - 1.609

The distance traveled during the sampling event reported in kilometers.

Column 18: Number of Observers - 1

The total number of observers participating in the sampling event. Usually 1.

Column 19: All Species Reported - 1

A critical field that separates eBird checklist data from most other observational datasets. Observers answer 'yes' to this question when they are reporting all species detected by sight and by ear to the best of their ability on a given checklist (sampling event). Observers answer 'no' to this question when they are only reporting a selection of species from an outing, usually the highlights or unusual birds. When observers report all species it allows us to understand more about detection probabilities, and given a large enough sample serves as a surrogate for absence data. (1 = yes; 0 = no).

Column 20: Approved - 1

The status of the record within the eBird data quality process. If "Approved", the record is deemed acceptable. If "Not Approved" the record has been deemed unacceptable by eBird’s review processes. The only current cases where "Not Approved" records are included in the dataset are as follows: Introduced/Exotic species; and unvetted data. In the case of Introduced/Exotic species the identification of the species is not usually in question, but many are set to "Not Approved" because their natural occurrence is questionable or not established. TL;DR: Our data should only have 1 in this column, but we are including it just to make sure.

Column 21: Latitude - 44.862

Latitude of the observation in decimal degrees rounded to only three decimal points.

Column 22: Longitude - -93.616

Longitude of the observation in decimal degrees rounded to only three decimal points.

The Habitat Section

The following columns describe the habitat of the observation. Using MODIS landcover imagery with the University of Maryland classification scheme, the location of each eBird observation is described from 0 to 100 for 14 landcover cases. Note that the columns in the habitat section sum to 100; each column essentially represents the portion of the piece of land covered by that particular habitat type. The Expert Score Section

Using the equation provided in the paper (linked above), we calculated the expert score for each user. In short, the “expert score” is the log-expected-value of the number of species observed by a given observer in one hour, determined by a gaussian additive mixture model. In our data, we give the log form and the exponentiated form for ease of interpretation. This will be useful for finding the lowest and highest quartiles to distinguish expert birders from less experienced birders.

Column 38: Expert Score (log form) - 2.671144192

Column 39: Expert Score (value) - 14.45650075

Column 40: Checklist Number - 14

This column increments by one for each additional log by a given person. This was used in calculating the expert score, and we thought it could be relevant in further analyses.
