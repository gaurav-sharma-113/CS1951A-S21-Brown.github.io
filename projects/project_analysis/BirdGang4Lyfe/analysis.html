Analysis: BirdGang4Lyfe

Hypothesis:

We predict that expert birders report sightings of common birds less often than non-experts do because they have a better ability to spot difficult-to-find birds and know where uncommon birds are located. As such, their desire to report more common birds diminishes.

Why we used our statistical tests:

We started with a series of linear regressions in order to get a sense of how the features were operating in relation to each other. For our first sense of the relationship between common/rare birds and expert score, we ran two linear regressions--one regressing the number of common birds sighted on expert score and another regressing the number of rare birds sighted on expert score--and got mind blowing results! Just kidding, they were very disappointing. Although the coefficients on expert scores were statistically significant in both regressions, the direction of the slopes refute our hypothesis. The regressions suggest that increasing the expert score of a birder leads to an increase in the number of rare birds sighted and the number of common birds sighted. To support our hypothesis, increasing the expert score should have led to a decrease in the number of common birds sighted, on average. Further, the r-squared value was only around 0.01 in both cases. We wanted to use regressions to start because they are relatively simple and effective for getting a sense of relationship. Another easy, quick start that we wanted to check the correlation between expert and novice birders for our 20 bird species (seen in Figure 9). The correlation charts, although at a more granular level, are in agreement with the regressions; being an expert is positively correlated with reporting rare and common birds.

Other tests:

After our initial considerations, we decided to dissect the data a bit more. We went species by species looking at the difference in proportions of expert and novice checklists containing that species, determining statistical significance through a series of two-proportion z-tests. However, these tests also show that experts report more of all types of species, rather than just rare birds.

Defining Success/Failure:

For our regression, we were looking for statistical significance and a sizable coefficient in the negative direction. By these metrics, our initial hypothesis failed. We had a significant (p-value below 0.05) coefficient, but that coefficient was positive, suggesting that having a higher expert score leads to reporting more of the common birds. For our two-proportion z-tests, statistical significance would require a smaller p-value because we were running the test multiple times in a row, thus increasing our chances of finding significance by luck. Thus, for those tests, we said that a p-value below 0.001 (about 3 sigma) would be significant.

Reasons behind our tests:

The p-values would help us initially confirm that our results are statistically significant, while a high r-squared value would confirm that the variation in the number of common and rare birds sighted was actually a result of the expert score fluctuating. The coefficient would show us the magnitude and direction of that fluctuation. These are all standard statistical measures.

Challenges:

We have so much data that we have had to limit it a lot. This was helpful to look at stuff quickly and to see a sample. But it does mean that we weren’t able to look at everything (which would take a very long time to analyze). In addition, we are looking only at the twenty most “common” species and twenty “rarest” species, as determined by a previous paper on this data. It is possible that the most common birds and rarest birds according to the paper are not the same as those in our data. We will need to explore this further.

Data Cleaning and Restructuring

We didn’t have to clean anything, but we did need to do some restructuring. We created columns for species. For every row/birding list, we mark whether or not each of our 40 species has been seen and the counts of the number of rare species and common species. We also created novice and expert indicator columns, which were for the top and bottom quartiles of the expert scores. For the categorical column State, we used a one-hot encoding to test for correlations. Finally, we cleaned up the date field to be split into day, month, and year to see if there were any correlations over specific time periods.

Results:

Since our initial analysis has a significant p-value in the positive direction and a low r-squared value (~0.01), our data leads us to believe that increasing a given birder’s expert score does not cause a decrease in reporting common birds. We therefore reject our initial hypothesis. Instead, we found that experts were more likely to sight both common and rare birds.This would explain why our initial hypothesis has proven to be incorrect.  

We have also initially found that the amount of hours spent birdwatching is more strongly correlated with the number of rare and common birds sighted. When our model includes this variable, our r-squared values are much higher (0.25-0.30, depending on the exact metric used). We will be assessing this finding further for our final deliverable.

As seen in Figure 9, the correlations between being a novice birder and seeing various common birds are not positive, as we would expect them to be. This leads us to believe that we are not looking at the most “common” birds after all. However, this would also mean that the most common birds in our dataset are different from those in the original paper from which we gathered the most common and rare birds, which is possible (as our data is from a later year and is very filtered down). This is something we will need to grapple with. If we run these tests with a different set of common and rare birds, chosen in some unbiased fashion, our results may differ significantly.

Figure 9: Correlations Between Various Bird Sightings and Being an Expert or Novice

We found that being an expert birdwatcher is positively correlated with sighting many of the rare birds on any given outing, whereas being a novice is negatively correlated with sighting many of the rare birds on any given outing. This is what we would expect, as novice birdwatchers should not be able to identify birds that experts can. Contrary to our hypothesis, being an expert birder also positively correlates with many common bird sightings, although there are more exceptions here than among the rare birds. Interestingly, being a novice birder is negatively correlated with sighting many of the common birds. Note, however, that those common birds that are positively correlated with being a novice are typically negatively correlated with being an expert. This data suggests that we may need to re-think which birds are considered common, as it was assumed that the novice birders would be able to identify them fairly easily (we used “common” and “rare” as determined in the original paper, but maybe we should change this). Such reconsideration could significantly affect our final conclusions. This figure requires some of the above description as supporting text for a final poster.

Figure 10: Two Proportion Z-Tests on Proportions of Checklists with Common Birds

We ran a two proportion z-test on the difference between proportions of expert and novice bird checklists with various common birds. Each test would tell us whether there was a significant difference between expert and novice birders in reporting various common birds. Because we ran the test multiple times, we would consider a p-value significant if it was below 0.001 (thus taking into account that we could have gotten lucky by nature of running the test multiple times). Contrary to our hypothesis, we found that experts were more likely to report seeing 16 of the 20 selected common birds. Of the 16 birds that experts were more likely to report, 13 proportion differences had significant p-values. Of the 4 birds that novices were more likely to report, 2 proportion differences had significant p-values. We have highlighted the birds that supported our hypothesis; all other birds do not support our hypothesis.This figure requires some of the above description as supporting text for a final poster.

Future Directions:

As noted previously, we have been led to believe that our common and rare bird categorizations may be off. In our upcoming analyses, we will break down the bird categories using a different objective metric to see how that affects our correlation with expert score. We will also see how including different variables in our regressions impacts the sightings of rare and common birds with our newly defined categories.

For our poster, we will also include some of the original work from the original paper for reference, to provide context to our own work.
