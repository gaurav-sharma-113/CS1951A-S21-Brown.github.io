The main purpose of our project is to understand the relationship between Airbnb listings and the real estate market in selected cities in the U.S. Specifically, our project looks at data from January 2015 to January 2020 from the following cities: Asheville, Austin, Boston, Chicago, Denver, Los Angeles, Nashville, New York, San Francisco and Seattle.

Our Hypothesis: We hypothesize that when the real estate market is successful in a particular neighborhood, the Airbnb market will also be successful.

The first step we took when addressing this hypothesis was to define what it means to be “successful” in a particular neighborhood. In terms of the Airbnb market, we defined “success” by the number of Airbnbs that are rented in a particular zip code (or, on a larger scale, city). In terms of the real estate market, we used the average price of homes per zip code to define a “successful” real estate market. That is, we interpret more expensive houses in a given zip code (or city) as an indicator of a successful neighborhood.

Using these definitions of success, our next step was to determine what type of test we wanted to run. We did research about t-tests, z-tests, chi-squared tests, correlation coefficients, and linear regressions. We ultimately decided against a t-test, z-test and chi-squared test because they did not fit well with our data. T-tests and z-tests are used to determine if there is a significant difference between the mean value of two groups, which does not make sense when comparing the number of Airbnbs with the average housing price. Chi-squared tests are used to test the relationship between two categorical variables, which does not apply to the variables we wanted to look at. Ultimately, we chose to run a correlation between our two variables: the number of Airbnbs and the average price of homes.

Interpretation of Results

From our correlation analysis comparing the number of Airbnbs with the average housing price by city, we did not find evidence to support our hypothesis. The correlation coefficient in a given year is between 0.15 and 0.30, which indicates a non existent (or very weak) correlation between the number of Airbnbs and the average price of homes in each city. Therefore, we did not find that a successful housing market may be correlated with a successful Airbnb market in a given city.

Interestingly, when we calculate the correlation between the number of Airbnbs and the average housing price for each zip code, the correlation values are between 0.4 and 0.5. These values indicate a slightly better (or stronger) correlation than when the data was aggregated by zip code. The p-value is also very low with values less than 10-19, indicating strong evidence against null hypothesis. However, these correlations still do not provide enough evidence for us to confidently claim that our hypothesis is supported.

It is important to discuss our confidence in our results so far. From our correlation analyses we found a very weak correlation among the number of Airbnbs with the average price of homes on both a city and zip code level. When we group the data by the city, we have less data to work with (i.e. only 10 data points) and the variation between the data points contributes significantly to the correlation value. When we group the data by zip code, we are able to pass in many more data points. However, our analysis demonstrates that there is a very weak correlation. These results make sense because the number of Airbnbs does not singularly influence the average housing price.
