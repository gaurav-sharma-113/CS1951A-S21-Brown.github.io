L.A.M.E. Data Analysis

We wanted to investigate how factors of success or popularity for films have changed over the past few decades. In particular, we were interested in how preferences for genre, seasonalty (in terms of release date) have changed as we predicted these were most critical in determining a movie's success. We hypothesize that seasonality plays a much greater role in the success of a film today than it has in previous decades, and also expect to see animation and action films become much more profitable because of improvements in technology and special effects.

STATS – Chi-Squared Tests

  • Why did you use this statistical test or ML algorithm? Which other tests did you consider or evaluate? How did you measure success or failure? Why that metric/value? What challenges did you face evaluating the model? Did you have to clean or restructure your data?
  • We considered the use of a t-test to look at the differences in numbers between time periods to see if they were statistically different from one another but decided that this did not capture an important issue which is that if the data did show statistically relevant differences between time periods how do we know that that is in fact due to a causal impact of our independent values on our dependent values. Instead we need to check for lack of independence between our dependent and independent values.
  • We chose to use a Chi-Squared Independence test to test whether our hypothesis was correct in predicting that both genre and seasonality were important factors of a film's success. We chose this test because we wanted to prove that both genre and seasonality had a statistically significant relationship to return on investment (ROI) or Rotten Tomatoes Rating.
  • In order to bypass complications of inflation in both our visualizations and Chi-Squared tests, we chose to add a return on investment (ROI) column to our data and use this in place of revenue to measure success.

Seasonality and ROI

Above we can see the chi-squared values of Seasonality vs. ROI for each time interval listed. For pre-1980 and 1980s-2000 we can see that there is a significance level far above .05 (5%), meaning seasonality seems to be independent from ROI during these periods. However, the post-2000 chi-square independence value drops significantly–to .1%–meaning seasonality and ROI certainly seem to be not independent of each other. This value drops even further–to .01%–when looking at all movies in our dataset, which seems to say that, overall, seasonality plays an important role in a movie's ultimate ROI.

Genre and ROI

The above chi-squared test shows the independence value of genre and ROI. Since the chi-squared value is below .05 (5%), it seems that genre and ROI are not independent of each other. VISUALIZATIONS

For our first visualizations we looked at how success in certain genres has shifted over the past 30 years based on both ROI and Rotten tomatoes ratings. We chose to look into both these factors to assess the difference between economic success and critics success. This is illustrated in the above nine line graphs comparing ROI or ratings vs. time (yearly) for each of the most represented genres in our data. In the above graph fluctuations in ROI and ratings from 1990 to the present for each genre are shown. While most (not all) show a general upward trend–likely because of a general increase in advertising and streaming ability via the internet–certain genres like Action, Romance, and Animation show much more dramatic spikes in present times (2010 and beyond).

We created graphs of season to median ROI over three different time periods in order to better visualize how return on investment varied by season across time as well as to get a better understanding of the data we were dealing with. Movies from the 1960-1979 time period have on average far higher ROI values than movies made from 1980 onward. This is likely because as this is a recent database, the only movies that were included from the 1960s and 70s were the ones that were major successes. Regardless, the bar graphs alone did not show a clear trend based on seasons alone. However, we thought it may be interesting to visualize which seasons had more profitable films given that, when considering our stats test, seasonality does seem to be a significant factor in ultimate ROI.
